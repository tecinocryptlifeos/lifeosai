Decision Guides

Practical frameworks for decisions that carry real consequences.

These concise guides provide a public learning layer around the LifeOS AI application. Use them before opening a decision conversation.

Guide 1: Define the decision correctly

Write the decision as one sentence: ‘I am deciding whether to…’ Then write the objective separately: ‘The result I need is…’ This distinction matters. A person may think the decision is whether to accept a job, while the real objective is stable income, professional growth or relocation. Once the objective is explicit, additional options become visible. Record the deadline, the facts already verified and the information still missing. Do not treat assumptions as facts.

Guide 2: Calculate the hidden cost

List the visible cost first, then inspect five less visible categories: time, attention, dependence, reputation and lost alternatives. Ask what must be delayed, who gains control over the outcome and what happens if the commitment fails. A decision with a small cash price can carry a large opportunity cost. Compare the full cost against the probable value, not against the most attractive promise.

Guide 3: Separate urgency from importance

Urgency is a time condition; importance is a consequence condition. Sellers, social pressure and fear can manufacture urgency without increasing importance. Before acting, identify who created the deadline, what changes when it expires and whether a smaller reversible step is available. Genuine emergencies require action, but many commercial and personal pressures become clearer after a deliberate pause.

Guide 4: Compare options with the same standard

Choose a common set of criteria before comparing options. Useful criteria include cost, expected benefit, risk, reversibility, time requirement, evidence quality and alignment with the main objective. Apply the same criteria to every option. Changing the standard to favour a preferred choice is not comparison; it is justification.

Guide 5: Use AI without surrendering judgement

Do not provide passwords, financial credentials, private identification records or information you are not authorised to disclose. Correct the AI when context is wrong. Verify important claims with reliable sources. For legal, medical, regulated financial, safety or emergency matters, use qualified professionals and relevant authorities. AI should expand the questions you consider—not remove your responsibility for the answer.

Guide 6: Choose the next responsible action

A decision does not always require a complete commitment. The next responsible action may be to request documentation, inspect a sample, speak to a professional, run a small trial or wait for missing evidence. Select the smallest action that materially reduces uncertainty without creating disproportionate exposure. Then reassess with the new information.
